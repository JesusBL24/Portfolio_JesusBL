About me

Born in 2003, my interest in video games started at the age of three, allowing me to internally develop an intuitive understanding of why different mechanics work and the objectives behind design decisions focused on the player experience . As a child, I also spent long evenings playing with LEGO, imagining stories and worlds that later inspired me to begin writing, programming, and cultivating a strong interest in storytelling and art.

I recently received my Bachelor's degree in Game Design and Development from Rey Juan Carlos University in Madrid. During these years, I studied game design, the video game production pipeline, and programming in C#, C++, javascript and Unity Engine . Furthermore, my desire for improvement led me to independently studying design and participating with colleagues in several game jams as a designer and storyteller (and even once as an art director). This experience has significantly strengthened my communication, team working, analysis, and problem-solving skills.

This year, my first multiplatform project, 'El Coco', was released, where I collaborated with the Recotechnology S.L. team as a Game Designer and Producer. My responsibilities included designing difficulty and progression systems, structuring the narrative, implementing levels and enemies, leading the team in my production role, and preparing the game pages for all launch platforms.

This is just the beginning; my passion for games and continuous desire to learn drive me to seek new horizons and further develop my professional skills.

Design Process

My studies and research have effectively improved my capability to engage with game design and manage the iterative process.

  • Stage 1: Define the problem

    Whether it is defining the core player experience, creating new mechanics and systems, or tuning existing ones, the first step is identifying the problem we need to solve in the current iteration.


    In the first stages of conceptualization, the objective should be defining our core experience. In this case, the problem could be stated as finding the right main mechanics and systems that match a single statement: a phrase that conveys the desired experience that the game should provide. Once development has started, iteration objectives range from creating new content and systems for the game to tuning and balancing various existing features.

  • Stage 2: Find the solution

    There are many solutions for each problem, but finding the correct one is all about analysis, exploring different ideas, and seeing the big picture of both video game and development needs.


    This stage is all about brainstorming and analyzing ideas by using risk identification and the 5 Pillars of Game Design
    (Clarity, Satisfaction, Response, Motivation, and Fantasy) to check if they enhance the player experience and if they can be developed correctly. When creating new game systems and mechanics, it is important to know design patterns and how to implement them, adapting existing ideas in an innovative way that suits the design needs.


    Once we have explored enough options, we choose a solution or a mix of several of them and analyze it once again to validate the final idea or look for another solution that can fit better into th current game.

  • Stage 3: Test the solution

    Implementing new features and mechanics starts with creating a quick prototype to find their weaknesses and strengths as soon as possible.


    To maintain good documentation, we first create a small one-pager summarising all the relevant information and objectives of the intended solution. Later, we create a small sample of the feature by using paper prototypes, Machinations, or video game engines with simple forms and scripts in a way that they can be easily tested and tunned.


    Once the prototype is ready, it should be tested with the necessary people. Whether it is the team or external testers depends on the context, but the target public should always be taken into account, and information recovery strategies must be prepared beforehand.

  • Stage 4: Evaluation

    After recovering all the necessary feedback, the information must be analyzed to determine if the solution can be added to the game, if any upgrades should be made, or if it should be discarded. Whether the solution is approved or not, the evaluation must be documented and used to determine new objectives in subsequent iterations.

  • Repeat

    By the end of the iteration, ideas should have been tested and features implemented. Now it is time to gather the team and look for possible upgrades to the process, identify new needs, and plan future features and iterations.
